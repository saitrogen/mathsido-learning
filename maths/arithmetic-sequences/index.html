The jump gets a name

The Jump Size: Common Difference

Goal: define the common difference d and learn how to find it.

The frog's jumps are all the same size. That size is the common difference, d.

d = any term − the term before it

Check it anywhere: 4 − 2 = 2  ·  6 − 4 = 2  ·  8 − 6 = 2. Same answer every time. Here d = +2.

Find d for 3, 7, 11, 15.

Downhill jumps

Negative Common Difference

Goal: get comfortable with decreasing sequences and negative d.

Subtract the same safe way — later term minus earlier term: 7 − 10 = −3. The minus sign just means “downhill”. And the stairs keep going: next comes 1 − 3 = −2, below zero!

100, 95, 90, … — what is d, and what's the next term?

⚠️ Warning sign

A Few Terms Can Lie

Goal: understand why 3, 5, 7, … isn't automatically arithmetic.

The same three numbers have two different futures. If the rule is “odd numbers”, next is 9. If it's “prime numbers”, next is 11. Three numbers alone can't promise the future — we must be told the rule (or told “this is arithmetic”). Then, and only then, can we predict.

💬 Try it: give 1, 2, 4 two different futures
Doubling rule → next is 8. Or jumps +1, +2, +3 → next is 7. Same start, different stories!

Geometry builds sequences

The Polygon Angle Ladder

Goal: see 180, 360, 540, … as arithmetic — and why.

Each new shape hides exactly one more triangle than the last, and every triangle is worth a 180° token. One more triangle = +180°. Geometry built us an arithmetic sequence: 180, 360, 540, 720, … with d = 180.

Angle sum for a 10-sided shape?

💡 Hint
A 10-sided shape slices into 8 triangles (always sides − 2).

Textbook problem, animated

The Perpendicular Staircase

Goal: see why equally spaced posts under a slanted line form an arithmetic sequence.

Draw a level dashed line from each post-top to the next post (the textbook's hint!). You get a row of identical right triangles: same base spacing, same slope ⇒ same rise. Each post = previous post + the same rise. Constant slope + equal spacing manufactures equal jumps.

Post 1 is 2 cm tall and each rise is 1.5 cm. Post 5 is…?

The Balance Scale

Goal: discover that (before) + (after) = 2 × middle — no algebra needed.

8⌒+412⌒+416
8 + 16 = 24   ⚖️   2 × 12 = 24   — the beam levels!

The why: 16 has exactly as much extra (+4) as 8 is missing (−4). Let 16 give its spare 4 to 8 — now both are 12. Two twelves! Outer neighbours average to the middle.

Trio: 5, □, 25. Balance the scale — what's the middle?

🪞 Mirror pairs

Pairs at the Mirror

Goal: discover 1st+6th = 2nd+5th = 3rd+4th.

All three pairs sum to 35! Why: walk both partners one step toward the mirror — one term grows by d, the other shrinks by d. The sum can't change.

In an arithmetic sequence, 2nd + 9th = 40. What is 5th + 6th?

💡 Hint
Compare position sums: 2 + 9 = 11 and 5 + 6 = 11.

Mirrors at work

Cracking a Problem with Symmetry

Goal: use pair symmetry on “first four terms sum to 100, 1st term is 10”.

Step 1 — pair up: positions (1,4) and (2,3) sit symmetrically. The 100-purse splits evenly: each pair sums to 50.

Step 2 — use the clue (1st term = 10): the 4th term = 50 − 10 = 40.

Step 3 — journey method: 1 → 4 is 3 jumps covering 30 → d = 10.

10⌒+1020⌒+1030⌒+1040

Check: 10+20+30+40 = 100 ✓. Symmetry turns one big fact into two friendly facts.

Textbook: 7th + 8th terms sum to 50. Sum of the first 14 terms?

💡 Hint
Pair 1&14, 2&13, 3&12 … every pair has position-sum 15 = 7+8, so every pair sums to 50. How many pairs?

d is one subtraction, done in the right order: later − earlier.

1) d of 3, 8, 13, …?

🔓 Solution
8 − 3 = 5.

2) d of 20, 14, 8, …?

💡 Hint
Later − earlier, even downhill.
🔓 Solution
14 − 20 = −6.

3) d of ½, 1, 1½, …? (the textbook's halves!)

🔓 Solution
1 − ½ = ½. Fractions are welcome.

4) First term 7, d = 4 — write four terms.

🔓 Solution
7, 11, 15, 19 (hop +4 each time).

5) A sequence contains … 50, 57 … as neighbours. Find d and the term after 57.

🔓 Solution
d = 57 − 50 = 7; next term = 57 + 7 = 64.

🏆 Reasoning, not calculation

Challenge Arena

Five advanced thinking questions. Visual hint first, full reasoning second.

C1 — Pentagon angles (textbook): the five angles of a pentagon form an arithmetic sequence. (i) The middle (3rd) angle? (ii) Can the smallest angle be 36°?

💡 Visual hint
Five levelling bars; a pentagon's angles total 540°.
🔓 Reasoning
(i) Middle = 540 ÷ 5 = 108°. (ii) If the smallest were 36°, it sits 72 below the middle, so the largest sits 72 above: 108 + 72 = 180° — a straight line, not a corner. Impossible.

C2 — The telltale remainder: why does EVERY term of 13, 24, 35, … leave remainder 2 on division by 11?

💡 Visual hint
Picture each term as a stack of 11-blocks plus a little 2-brick on top.
🔓 Reasoning
Terms differ by whole 11-blocks; adding 11-blocks never touches the 2-brick. The remainder is frozen.

C3 — One fact, many sequences: the 2nd term is 5. Draw three different sequences that fit. What does EVERY such sequence share?

💡 Visual hint
A seesaw hinged at (position 2, value 5) — the line can tilt any way.
🔓 Reasoning
e.g. 4,5,6 · 3,5,7 · 6,5,4. They all share 1st + 3rd = 10 (= 2 × the middle). One known term pins a point, not a line.

C4 — Mirror logic (textbook): 11th + 21st terms sum to 80. Find the 16th term — without knowing the sequence!

💡 Visual hint
Where does the mirror between positions 11 and 21 stand? Exactly at 16.
🔓 Reasoning
The 16th is the exact middle of the pair, so it's the average: 80 ÷ 2 = 40.

C5 — True or false court: “If a + c = 2b, then a, b, c are consecutive terms of some arithmetic sequence.”

💡 Visual hint
Build the balance scale backwards.
🔓 Reasoning
TRUE. Set d = b − a; then a + c = 2b forces c = b + d automatically. The balance condition IS the arithmetic condition.
